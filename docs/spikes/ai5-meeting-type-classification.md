# AI5 spike: meeting-type classification

Spike, 2026-07-11. **Measured and resolved 2026-07-23: NO-GO on a permanent `MeetingSummary.meeting_type` field.** Instruments: `mp classify-meetings` (the prototype labeller, kept) and [`ai5_agreement.py`](./ai5_agreement.py) (the reducer that produced [`ai5-classification-results.json`](./ai5-classification-results.json)).

## Question

Competitors mostly do not auto-detect meeting type (only MeetGeek does). Would on-device type labels cluster the owner's library usefully enough to earn a permanent schema field and drive grouping / digests? The spike's acceptance was explicit: prototype a throwaway classifier, show the labels on a real library, return a recommendation, and land no schema change until that decision.

## What was blocked, and what unblocked it

The prototype shipped 2026-07-11 but the judgement could not be made: the library on this Mac held **2 test meetings, both landing `client`**. Too thin to judge a 10-type taxonomy. By 2026-07-23 the library had filled to **193 summarized meetings**, so both legs were run for real.

## Method

Two independent labellers over the same fixed 10-type taxonomy, same 193 meetings:

- **Heuristic** (default, zero-egress): first-match keyword rules over the summary title + workflow name, then attendee-count fallbacks.
- **Local LLM**: one `engine.complete_text` call per meeting, 12 output tokens, snapped to the taxonomy.

The owner's real config is `backend = "anthropic"`, so a plain `--llm` would have shipped 193 real meeting titles to the cloud. The spike's question is about *on-device* labels, so the run was forced onto the local backend (`effective_backend` returned `local`, Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct-4bit on the already-warm mlx server). Zero egress, and it exercises the path the AI/DV standing rule cares about.

**Agreement between two independent labellers is the measurement.** Neither is ground truth, and hand-labelling 193 meetings was not on offer. Where two methods with completely different failure modes both land on the same bucket, that bucket is real; where they diverge, the *taxonomy* is not separable from the signals available, whichever one you would have believed.

## Result: 1 of 10 types is reliable, 4 of 10 have zero overlap

Overall agreement is **88/193 = 45.6%**. That headline is itself flattering, because the mass sits in one easy bucket. The per-label overlap (Jaccard, heuristic set vs LLM set) is the real picture:

| Type | heuristic | llm | both | Jaccard |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| `standup` | 50 | 49 | 48 | **94.1%** |
| `review` | 24 | 42 | 19 | 40.4% |
| `all_hands` | 3 | 2 | 1 | 25.0% |
| `planning` | 10 | 27 | 7 | 23.3% |
| `one_on_one` | 33 | 48 | 8 | **11.0%** |
| `other` | 69 | 13 | 5 | 6.5% |
| `client` | 3 | 1 | 0 | **0.0%** |
| `brainstorm` | 1 | 4 | 0 | **0.0%** |
| `retro` | 0 | 3 | 0 | **0.0%** |
| `interview` | 0 | 4 | 0 | **0.0%** |

`standup` separates cleanly and nothing else does. Four types have literally no meeting both labellers agree on. Two of them (`retro`, `interview`) the heuristic never assigns at all across 193 meetings.

## Four reasons the signal is not there

**1. The classified text is not the meeting's name.** `summary["title"]` is the *summarizer's invented title*, generated by the LLM that wrote the summary. So the heuristic keyword-matches over model prose, and the `--llm` leg asks a small local model to re-classify a large cloud model's paraphrase. Neither labeller ever sees what the meeting was actually called. Any type field built this way inherits the summarizer's phrasing drift as a silent input.

**2. The workflow tag carries almost no signal.** Library-wide there are three workflow values: `Personal` (95), `General` (52), `Client work` (1), plus 45 meetings with none. `Personal` alone spreads across seven of the ten types. A per-meeting classifier cannot lean on it.

**3. The attendee count is unreliable at the source.** It is `len(summary["attendees"])`, which is diarization-derived, not a roster. 31 meetings report a single attendee and only 13 report exactly 2 in a library where the largest single keyword cluster is a team standup. The attendee-count fallbacks (`==2` to `one_on_one`, `>=9` to `all_hands`) rest on a number that is measuring speaker separation, not who was in the room.

**4. The LLM does not abstain; it relabels the garbage bucket.** The heuristic leaves 69 meetings (35.8%) in `other`. The LLM's `other` is only 13 (6.7%), which looks like a large improvement and is not: it moved 36 of those 69 into `one_on_one`. Its `one_on_one` total is 48, yet on the 13 meetings that genuinely have 2 attendees it says `one_on_one` only 5 times. `one_on_one` is simply where that model puts a meeting it cannot type, which is exactly the failure a permanent field must not encode.

## One defect found and fixed

The heuristic's `one_on_one` rule matched `\bsync\b`. On the real library that single pattern produced **22 of the 33** `one_on_one` labels, and **20 of 33** `one_on_one` meetings had 4 to 12 attendees ("Team Sync: Releases, 5.11 Issues & Compliance" at 12, "Dev Sync: Auth Rework" at 5). `sync` names a *cadence*, not a headcount. The pattern is removed and pinned by a test.

Removing it is honest but does not rescue the verdict, which is the point worth recording: agreement moves 45.6% to **47.7%**, because the 22 meetings simply fall back into `other` (35.8% to 43.0%). The problem is the available signal, not one bad regex.

## Incidental finding: the library contains non-meetings

Roughly 8 rows in the `other` bucket are not meetings: "Test Recording", "Call Quality Test Recording", "Voicemail Recording - No Meeting Content", "No Substantive Meeting Content", "Minimal/Empty Meeting Recording". A type field would be dutifully labelling junk. This is a Library-hygiene observation, not an AI5 deliverable, and is left unfiled unless the owner wants it.

## Verdict

**NO-GO. No `MeetingSummary.meeting_type` field.** Acceptance is honored: the prototype ran on the real library and the recommendation is not to adopt. One reliable bucket out of ten does not earn a permanent schema field, a publisher change, and a migration, and the one bucket that does separate (`standup`) is already reachable by typing "standup" into the UX16 full-text search, which searches the real text rather than a derived guess.

The specific thing not to build: a field that is 94% right on a quarter of the library, coin-flip on another quarter, and silently wrong on the rest, presented in the UI with the same confidence everywhere. Grouping the Library by such a field would make the low-agreement types look like categories rather than guesses.

**If it is ever revisited, the gate is a better input, not a better classifier.** Both labellers were reading a generated title because there is no real one. The unlock is a genuine meeting title, which means the calendar; CAL1 measured that as structurally blind on this Mac (the owner's Outlook/Teams calendar is not connected to macOS EventKit), so this stays shut until that changes. A larger local model is not the unlock: the 7B was not wrong through weakness, it was guessing because the input does not contain the answer.

## What this unblocks

**AI9** ("Learned workflow routing") carried "Gated on the AI5 owner verdict". That gate is now resolved, and resolved in a way that does not block AI9: AI9's signal is the WF8 reassignment pairs, actual owner corrections of a real workflow assignment, which is a strictly better-grounded label than an inferred type. AI9 should not take a dependency on `meeting_type`; nothing here says workflow suggestion is a bad idea, only that a derived type field is.

`mp classify-meetings` is kept as the owner-dev harness that produced this (de-CLI exempt, spike class). Re-running it after the library grows or after a calendar lands is a one-command way to re-test the verdict.
